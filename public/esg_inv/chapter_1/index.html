Basic Pricing Equation

Derivation

Assume the current asset price is $p_{t}$, and investors are making choices between consuming today or investing and consuming tomorrow. The utility function of an investor is given by:

$$U(c_{t},c_{t+1})=U(c_{t})+\beta E_{t}[U(c_{t+1})]$$

where $U(c_{t})=\frac{1}{1-\gamma}c_{t}^{1-\gamma}$, $\beta$ is the *subjective discount factor*, it means the level of impatience by investors. When $\gamma\rightarrow1$, we have $U(c_{t})\rightarrow Ln(c_{t})$.

So, the decision of investors is based on:

$$\begin{array}{c} \underset{\xi}{max}{U(c_{t})+\beta E_{t}[U(c_{t+1})]}\
s.t.\thinspace c_{t}=e_{t}-p_{t}\xi\
c_{t+1}=e_{t+1}-x_{t+1}\xi \end{array}$$

where $e_{t}$ means the original consumption level, $x_{t+1}$ is the payoff at time $t+1$, $x_{t+1}=p_{t+1}+d_{t+1}$( $p_{t+1}$ is the price of the asset, $d_{t+1}$ is the dividend at time $t+1$) The F.O.D condition is given by

$$p_{t}U^{'}(c_{t})=E_{t}[\beta U^{'}(c_{t+1})x_{t+1}]$$

$p_{t}U^{'}(c_{t})$ means the utility loss of buying an asset with price $p_{t}$; $E_{t}[\beta U^{'}(c_{t+1})X_{t+1}]$ means the expected utility gain from future investment payoff. Then we have:

$$p_{t}=E_{t}[\beta\frac{U^{'}(c_{t+1})}{U^{'}(c_{t})}x_{t+1}]\label{eq:central}$$

Eq.([eq:central]{reference-type=“ref” reference=“eq:central”}) is called “central asset pricing formula”.

Marginal Rate of Substitution/ Stochastic Discount Factor

We define the Stochastic Discount Factor as:

$$m_{t+1}=\beta\frac{U^{'}(c_{t+1})}{U^{'}(c_{t})}$$

$m_{t+1}$ can be regarded as “the marginal rate of substitution” or the “pricing kernel/change of measure/state price density”. Then Eq.([eq:central]{reference-type=“ref” reference=“eq:central”}) becomes:

$$p_{t}=E_{t}[m_{t+1}x_{t+1}]\label{eq:central-1}$$

Classic Issues in Finance

All About $R_{f}$

Continue with Eq.([eq:central]{reference-type=“ref” reference=“eq:central”}), if we set $p_{t}=1$, then the payoff for the next period is $x_{t+1}=R_{t+1}$, that is, we will have

$$1=E_{t}[m_{t+1}R_{t+1}]$$

If the return is the risk-free rate, it is known ahead of time, so the equation becomes $1=E_{t}[m_{t+1}]R_{f}$, and thus our definition of risk-free rate is:

$$R_{f}=\frac{1}{E[m]}\label{eq:risk_free_def}$$

If we use the power utility $U(c)=c^{-\gamma}$, then the risk-free rate becomes:

$$R_{f}=\frac{1}{\beta}\left(\frac{c_{t+1}}{c_{t}}\right)^{\gamma}\label{eq:risk_free}$$

Eq. ([eq:risk_free]{reference-type=“ref” reference=“eq:risk_free”}) tells us three things:

  • First, the lower the beta, the higher the risk-free rate. That is, the more people get impatient about the future consumption ($\frac{1}{\beta}\uparrow$), the higher the return.

  • Second, the higher $\frac{c_{t+1}}{c_{t}}$, the higher the risk-free rate. In other words, if the consumption growth becomes larger, consumer should have higher return for compensation.

  • Third, the higher the $\gamma$ the higher the return, meaning that when people get more risk averse, there should be higher return for compensation of taste.

::: center Adding Some More Uncertainty :::

If there is uncertainty, then the E(.) can not be taken out, and thus Eq. ([eq:risk_free]{reference-type=“ref” reference=“eq:risk_free”}) becomes:

$$\begin{array}{c} R_{f}=\frac{1}{\beta}\frac{1}{E\left(\frac{c_{t+1}}{c_{t}}\right)^{-\gamma}}=\frac{1}{\beta}\left{ E_{t}\left[exp\left(-\gamma ln\frac{c_{t}}{c_{t+1}}\right)\right]\right} ^{-1}\
=exp(\delta)\left{ E_{t}\left[exp\left(-\gamma ln\Delta c_{t+1}\right)\right]\right} ^{-1} \end{array}\label{eq:rfuncertain}$$

where $\frac{1}{\beta}=exp(\delta)$ and $\Delta c_{t+1}=\frac{c_{t}}{c_{t+1}}$. $exp(.)$ means exponential. To further derive the equation, we have to use some characteristic of a log-normal distribution. If, for example, $c_{t}$ belongs to log-normal distribution, then we have:

$$E_{t}(exp(c_{t}))=exp\left[E_{t}c_{t}+\frac{1}{2}var(c_{t}\text{)}\right]\label{eq:lognormal}$$

Applying Eq. ([eq:lognormal]{reference-type=“ref” reference=“eq:lognormal”}) to Eq. ([eq:rfuncertain]{reference-type=“ref” reference=“eq:rfuncertain”}) we have:

$$R_{f}=exp(\delta)\left{ exp\left[-\gamma E_{t}\left(ln\Delta c_{t+1}\right)+\frac{1}{2}\gamma^{2}var_{t}(ln\Delta c_{t+1})\right]\right} ^{-1}$$

Take logarithm to both sides, we then have:

$$r_{f}=\delta+\gamma E_{t}\left(ln\Delta c_{t+1}\right)-\frac{1}{2}\gamma^{2}var_{t}(ln\Delta c_{t+1})\label{eq:risk_uncertain}$$

Note that when $R_{f}$ has values like 1.05 or 1.03, $r_{f}=lnR_{f}$ then becomes the normal interest return that we usually see. $var_{t}(ln\Delta c_{t+1})$ is called the “*precautionary savings*”, which means the volatility of consumption growth. The higher the precautionary savings, the less possible people want to consume and more likely people want to save. Therefore the interest rate is lower (because people all put money into banks and thus the interest rate becomes lower). Apart from this, Eq. ([eq:risk_uncertain]{reference-type=“ref” reference=“eq:risk_uncertain”}) have the same conclusion as before.

Risk Corrections

Based on the discussion above about the risk-free rate, we want to further explore the relationship between the price and the interest rate. We start from the basic asset pricing formula Eq.([eq:central]{reference-type=“ref” reference=“eq:central”}), by considering the covariance between the discount rate and the payoff: $cov(m,x)=E(mx)-E(m)E(x)$. So that we have

$$p=E(mx)=E(m)E(x)+cov(m,x)$$

$cov(m,x)$ is called the “risk adjustment”. Applying Eq. ([eq:risk_free_def]{reference-type=“ref” reference=“eq:risk_free_def”}) and the definition of $m$, $m=\frac{U^{'}(c_{t+1})}{U^{'}(c_{t})}$, we have:
